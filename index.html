Darfur War

Posted on January 31, 2003 by Mushon Zer-Aviv
Reply

Secretary of State Colin Powell called the Darfur conflict genocide and the worst humanitarian crisis of the 21st century as evidenced by the 400,000 lives lost, the destruction of villages of targeted ethnic groups, the 3,000,000 displaced and widespread incidents of sexual violence and malnutrition. Save Darfur, an alliance of over 100 faith-based, humanitarian and human rights organizations gets formed to raise public awareness and mobilize a massive response to the atrocities.

Chicago Climate Exchange

Posted on January 31, 2003 by Mushon Zer-Aviv
Reply

The now defunct Chicago Climate Exchange (CCX) was North America’s only voluntary, legally binding greenhouse gas (GHG) reduction and trading system for emission sources and offset projects in North America and Brazil. The exchange had more than 400 members ranging from corporations to state and municipalities such as Oakland and Chicago, to educational institutions, to farmers and their organizations.

Global Fund

Posted on January 31, 2002 by Mushon Zer-Aviv
Reply

The Global Fund to Fight AIDS, Tuberculosis and Malaria was formed to attract and disburse resources to prevent and treat diseases. This is a unique example of a public-private partnership. Countries implement health programs based on their priorities and the Global Fund provides financing from government, the private sector, and the philanthropic sector based on programs’ ability to achieve verifiable results. The Bill and Melinda Gates Foundation was one of the first to provide seed money for this initiative.

MDGs

Posted on January 31, 2000 by Mushon Zer-Aviv
Reply

All world leaders present at the Millenium Summit adopted the United Nations Millennium Declaration asserting that every individual has the right to dignity, freedom, equality, a basic standard of living that includes freedom from hunger and violence, and encourages tolerance and solidarity. MDG’s were established to meet these ideals within a fifteen-year time frame with objectives that included eradicating extreme poverty, reducing child mortality rates, fighting disease epidemics such as AIDS, and developing a global partnership for development. The MDG’s were ambitious given the strict time table for goal accomplishment.

Social Enterprise

Posted on January 31, 2000 by Mushon Zer-Aviv
Reply

The concept of social enterprise emerges, defining a social enterprise as an organization or venture that achieves its primary social or environmental mission using business methods. Social enterprises are designed to build a more just, sustainable world by applying market-based strategies to today’s social problems. The Vermont legistlature further endorsed the idea by recognizing L3Cs, a variation of a limited-liability company, that would operate like a for-profit business generating at least modest profits, but its primary aim would be to offer significant social benefits.

Charter School Movement

Posted on January 31, 1991 by Mushon Zer-Aviv
Reply

Charter school legislation was first enacted in Minnesota in response to a desire to create distinctive schools that would provide choices and greater accountability and flexibility to parents and students. Support for charter schools and school choice in general sometimes transcended party, racial, and economic lines as members of the public looked to market-based efforts to reform education. In many instances, the charter movement was seen as an important complement to community revitalization efforts focused on promoting business and commerical development and other neighborhood improvement initiatives.

Bono

Posted on January 31, 1986 by Mushon Zer-Aviv
Reply

The Conspiracy of Hope Tour brought together some of the most famous artists on the planet at the time – including U2, Sting, and Bryan Adams – for a series of benefit concerts across the U.S. to raise awareness of human rights and promote recognition of Amnesty International’s work during its 25th anniversary. Subsequently known as the Human Rights Concerts, the events led to a flurry of interest in Amnesty International – one month following the tour, US membership had increased by 45,000.

Ethiopian famine

Posted on January 25, 1983 by Mushon Zer-Aviv
Reply

Fueled by civil war and insurgency, the Ethiopian famine was the worst famine to hit the country in more than a century and took more than 400,000 lives. BBC’s reporting of the crisis led to several efforts such as Live Aid, Band-aid, and USA for Africa to raise money and awareness through music. USA for Africa held a benefit event, Hands Across America where 7,000,000 people made $10 contributions and joined hands in a human chain across the continental U.S. Band-Aid, a group of British and Irish musicians released the song, “Do They Know It’s Christmas?” and also brought attention to the need for comprehensive knowledge and solutions to address the complex crisis.

1910's

Woodrow Wilson

Posted on January 19, 1913 by Mushon Zer-Aviv
Reply

The 28th U.S. President and former President of Princeton University was one of the first government officials to actually study public administration. Wilson is known for introducing the idea of a League of Nations, whose principal mission was to maintain world peace. Its primary goals, as stated in its Covenant, included preventing war through collective security and disarmament, and settling international disputes through negotiation and arbitration. Other issues in this and related treaties included labor conditions, just treatment of native inhabitants, human and drug trafficking, arms trade, global health, prisoners of war, and protection of minorities in Europe.

Progressive Movement

Posted on January 18, 1890 by Mushon Zer-Aviv
Reply

The Progressive movement of the late 1800s sought to eliminate inefficiency and promote scientific solutions to waste and corruption. Progressives, including Jane Addams, Woodrow Wilson, and Williams Jennings Bryan, encouraged modernization and fought for cleaner food and water, the right to vote for women, and education reform. However, some conservatives disagreed with the growth of government that Progressivism required, especially regarding the prohibition of alcohol and the breakup of corporate monopolies.

Abolitionist movement

Posted on January 31, 1787 by Mushon Zer-Aviv

The federal government prohibited slavery in the Northwest territory, building on the Quakers’ profession that slaveholding was incompatible with Christian piety. The creation of the Committee for the Abolition of the Slave Trade was the beginning of a movement of tens of thousands of Americans working to condemn slavery on moral grounds.
